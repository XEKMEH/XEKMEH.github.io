12 Things You MUST Know About The U.S. Border

  • 1. Operation Gatekeeper

    Operation Gatekeeper, which was an immigration policy based on deterrence, through the increase of security and militarization of the border through means of fences, border patrol vehicles, helicopters, and increased personnel working the border. Through increasing security and deterrence of undocumented immigrants at the border, the United States became a country much more difficult to access from the southern border. The increase of security and militarization lead to the numbers of migrant deaths to grow dramatically. As seen in the table above, the number of deaths of undocumented immigrants increased by 474% between 1996 and 2000 (Cornelius, 669) which shows the impact of Operation Gatekeeper and Operation Hold the Line on the undocumented immigrants attempting to cross the border. Through making the border more dangerous to cross illegally, more deaths occur. Today, the border is still highly militarized, with very little focus on search and rescue, and the increase funds to border patrol being used to increase the number of personnel at the border daily (Dowd, 1).

  • 2. Operation Hold The Line

    Operation Hold the Line was a measure taken by border patrols upon witnessing a large number of illegal immigrants crossing the border. The head of the El Paso border patrol ordered a human and vehicle blockade. Unlike policies that preceded, Operation Hold the Line sought to prevent illegal immigration rather than finding the trespassers and deporting them after they have already crossed. The preventative action had both positive and negative impacts, depending on what perspective you took on. For the U.S., this operation saw improvements in the number of those attempting to cross over along the El Paso region. For those attempting to cross, the journey became even more treacherous. With increased border security in this region, immigrants sought out more deserted areas, which resulted in more deaths.

  • 3. Timeline for Operations Gatekeeper and Hold the Line

    In July of 1993, Silvestre Reyes takes over command of the border in El Paso, Texas (Martin 1). El Paso was a popular entry point because of its urban infrastructure and close proximity to the city of Juarez, Mexico, which were factors in making it safer to cross when compared to alternative routes through the desert. While responsible for the border region, Reyes began planning for Operation Hold-the-Line, which will utilize a blockade over a 20 mile region of the El Paso-Juarez border (Martin 1). The blockade officially took place on September 19, 1993 with the deployment of 400 border patrol agents, who were tasked with patrols of the region and repairs to the fences along the border (Martin 1). Blockade formally ended on October 2, 1993 after two weeks of action, but border patrol still remains tight in that region. Operation Gatekeeper was employed a year later along the Tijuana, San Diego border, the busiest land crossing for illegal immigrants into the United States. One major difference between this Gatekeeper and Hold-the-Line is that Gatekeeper was employed as a national strategy for border control, and signed into action by President Clinton on October 1, 1994 (Gatekeeper 1). As for both of these operations, despite their formal end, tough border patrols remained as a remnant from this era.

  • 4. U.S. Immigration Law and Economic Policies Spike Illegal Immigration

    Prior to the mid-1960s approximately a half million Mexicans, mostly temporary Bracero workers, migrated legally to America each year. Visa caps did not exist and Mexicans migrated relatively freely in and out of the country to meet labor needs (Massey and Pren).

    This changed when Congress ended the Bracero program and passed the Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965, which placed a cap of 20,000 visas per country, per year. After decades of relatively free migration between Mexico and the U.S, the “migratory flows did not disappear but simply continued without authorization or documentation” (Massey and Pren 5).

    Around this time, guided by neoliberal ideology, powerful U.S corporations and the United States government pushed for free trade zones in Panama, Puerto Rico, Mexico, and the Caribbean Basin (Gonzalez). Hundreds of factories, called maquiladoras, sprang up along the Mexico/American border and in the Caribbean. Corporations enjoyed cheap foreign labor, tax breaks, and loose regulations. The factories employed mostly women, upsetting traditional social order and contributing to high male unemployment (Gonzalez 257). The maquilas towns were poor, crowded, and work conditions were harsh. While U.S. corporations reaped large profits and Americans enjoyed cheap products, Latino workers saw a steady decline in their wages. Many fled to the United States to escape unemployment, and to seek higher wages and a better quality of life.

  • 5. U.S Proxy Wars Increase Illegal Immigration

    The Cold War saw many Central American countries propelled into the throes of civil war and chaos. The United States government, fearing the spread of communism, sponsored military coups, assassinations, covert CIA operations, and provided weapons to dictators in El Salvador, Nicaragua, Honduras, and Chili. As the region spiraled into chaos, people fled their homes for the safety of the United States, the same country whose “direct military and economic interventions” (Gonzalez 129) caused or escalated the conditions they were fleeing. The United States “refused to assist the thousands who streaming across the Mexican border to escape [the] terror” (Gonzalez 132) leaving illegal entry one of their few options.

  • 6. Proposition 187: Sending Out An SOS

    On November 8th 1994, 59% of California voters voted to pass Proposition 187 which made undocumented immigrants ineligible for public social services, public health, and access to the public school system (Cacho, 389). This was a huge contradiction on immigration because at the time, the United States needed immigrant workers but decide to worsen living conditions while working in the U.S. This was in answer to the Latino threat that was growing in the west coast. This forced many people to live together in one little apartment as well as children frightened they would be kicked out of school or turned in by their teachers. Overall, this proposition separated “undocumented immigrants” from “the people of California” (Cacho, 395).

  • 7. The Latino Threat

    The Latino Threat was the colluded work of the media throughout the later part of the twentieth century. This narrative grew larger during the Bracero Program and is still current today as news sources deem Latinos to be taking “our” jobs. There were three main claims that explained how Mexican immigrants were invading the United States. Latinos were believed to want to reconquer the south west part of the United States and return it to Mexican control. They were also deemed unable to learn english, a reproductive threat, unchanging and immutable, ect. To combat with this threat, the U.S. government enforced laws such as Proposition 187. They also build walls, both physical and human to try and slow down this “invasion”. This was another attempt by the United States to overpower a minority just like they did with the “Puerto Rican Problem” (Briggs, 165).

  • 8. Defensive Tactics

    The main tactic used in Operations Hold-the-Line was a heavily armed blockade, manned by 400 agents. Border patrol agents were given military weapons and vehicles to deter immigrants away from the urban areas of the border, and into the treacherous desert terrain (Martin 1). During Operation Gatekeeper, border patrol agent presence in that area ballooned up to 11,000 agents by 2005, and highly advanced surveillance equipment was employed to more effectively seek out and detain those trying to cross from Tijuana into San Diego (Gatekeeper 1). Pre Existing walls and fences were heavily fortified making crossings even more difficult. The purpose of these tactics was to deter illegal migrants from crossing in urban sections of the border, this would force them to take dangerous routes through the desert, which was thought to discourage them from trying to cross in the first place. However, this was not the case, with many migrants choosing to take the chance through the desert, resulting in numerous deaths as a result of the operations.

  • 9. Immigrant Death Statistics

    Due to increased border enforcement and different tactics used in the two major operations, apprehensions at the border declined in 93, but rose dramatically the following year, especially in San Diego and in El Paso. This enforcement deterred the flow of unauthorized immigration into more rural areas where the immigrants are met with harsh environment. Deaths due to heat and cold increased 400% from 1993 to 1995, and deaths due to drowning rose by 160% from 1996 to 1998(Karl 23). Deaths due to environmental conditions are now the most common cause of death for immigrants at the southern border. The number of motor vehicle accidents have also increased as a result of change in the border patrol operations, whereas the count for auto-pedestrian accidents, train accidents, suffocation, and homicides have maintained or have decreased with the reinforcement . This statistics are far different than that of US residents, where mortality rate from heat exposure due to weather conditions for Americans under 55 are less than one per million(Karl 34), much less than the mortality rate from homicides or vehicular accidents.

  • 10. Changes in Border Patrol Over Time

    Before Operation Gatekeeper and Operation Hold the Line, the borders were not militarized. The ideas of Operation Gatekeeper and Operation Hold the Line were to prevent and intercept illegal immigration as it was occuring at the border, whereas before, immigration policies focused on tracking down and deporting undocumented immigrants who were already in the United States. In 1904, the US-Mexican border first began to be patrolled, by 75 officers on horseback (Meyers, 1). By 1930, due to the Immigration Act of 1924 and the increased concern during World War II, immigration officers totaled up to be about 900. During the 1930’s, due to the Great Depression, the need for border patrol decreased. From the years 1942 to about 1965, most migration into the United States occurred through the Mexican Bracero program. By the 1970’s there was a new found need for immigration patrol, because of the quotas being reached. By 1977, Congress funded the entrance of 2,580 new Border Patrol staff (Meyers, 1) which doubled by 1987. More money was being funneled into Border Patrol programs, at the request of the Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986 (IRCA). More than 57% of the finances of IRCA went into the Border Patrol at the Southern border, with the money being spent on helicopters, night vision goggles, and surveillance equipment (Meyers, 1). The Immigration Act of 1990 led to another increase of Border Patrol Agents, and the construction of a 10 foot tall steel wall along the San Diego-Tijuana border (Meyers, 1). By 1992, just before Operation Hold the Line (Operation Blockade), Border Patrol’s funding had reached $325 million (Meyers, 1). Through “targeted enforcement” and “prevention through deterrence,” the policies of Border Patrol shifted to confront the issue of undocumented immigration before the immigrants entered the United States. This enforcement policy has only grown since 1993.

  • 11. National Impacts

    The Immigration and naturalization services nearly tripled from 1993 to 1999, and Border Patrol, under INS jurisdiction, saw 150% increase in its budget during the same period and the number of agents doubled, reaching 8,200 in 1999 (Hondagneu-Sotelo 141). This in turn caused more apprehension at the southern border. The success of Border Patrol resulted in aggressive pace and the attitude of restrictive policies issued on immigration from 1995, to 6 policies from 1965 to 1995, and 8 in 1996 to 2016(Massey and Pren 12). The new policies were also announced and taken with much press coverage than before. In April 2005, the Minuteman project started for civilian volunteers to patrol the Arizona-Mexico border. This project also aimed to focus media attention on the border issues and shape public opinion and influence immigration policies(Chavez 135). The public’s anti immigration opinions formulated through these tactics are then made back into more restrictive operations and policies, which increases Border Patrol’s enforcement, which further increases apprehensions(Massey and Pren 13). The Militarization of Border Patrol fueled by this feedback loop results in more immigrant deaths across the border and many human and civil rights violations committed by law enforcement officials such as rape, extortion, physical and psychological abuse, separation of families, and murder(Hondagneu-Sotelo 142).
